# Design Doc: DopeCart — A Parody Ordering App for Dopamine Release

## 1. Working Title

**DopeCart**

Alternative names:

* BhookhMart
* KalOrder
* RegretCart
* Dopamine Cart
* ParodyMart
* Impulse Cart
* Order Mat Kar

## 2. One-Line Pitch

A parody ordering app where users can browse late-night snacks, build a cart, and “place” an order that never arrives — giving them the dopamine hit of ordering without spending money, eating junk, or regretting it the next morning.

## 3. Product Concept

DopeCart looks and feels like a fun quick-commerce experience, but it does not deliver anything. Users browse familiar impulse items like chips, Coke Zero, ice cream, chocolates, Maggi, momos, energy drinks, biscuits, and random “why am I buying this?” products.

The user adds items to a cart. At checkout, instead of placing a real order, the app gives a satisfying parody confirmation:

> “Order cancelled successfully. You saved ₹486, 1,240 calories, and tomorrow-morning regret.”

The product exists to satisfy the ritual of ordering: browsing, choosing, adding to cart, seeing delivery promises, and getting a receipt — without the consequence.

## 4. Core Insight

People often do not only want the product. They want the feeling of:

* browsing
* choosing
* treating themselves
* breaking boredom
* reacting to stress
* feeling in control for a few minutes
* getting a tiny reward at night

Quick-commerce apps make the path from craving to purchase extremely short. DopeCart inserts a playful substitute between craving and actual spending.

The product is not anti-food, anti-shopping, or moralistic. It is a playful intervention for the moment when someone knows they are probably about to make an unnecessary impulse order.

## 5. Target Users

### Primary Users

Urban Indian users who frequently open Blinkit, Instamart, Zepto, or food delivery apps late at night out of boredom, craving, stress, or habit.

Likely segments:

1. **Students and hostel residents**

   * Late-night cravings
   * Low budgets
   * Meme-heavy sharing behavior

2. **Tech workers and young professionals**

   * Stress ordering after work
   * Sedentary lifestyle
   * Weight-loss guilt
   * High quick-commerce access

3. **Fitness / weight-loss users**

   * Want to avoid chips, ice cream, sugary drinks, chocolate, etc.
   * Need a fun replacement for impulse ordering

4. **Bored late-night scrollers**

   * Open apps without real hunger
   * Need a small dopamine loop

5. **Meme page audience**

   * Likely to share receipts
   * Enjoy Hinglish, self-roast, and relatable urban humor

## 6. User Problem

Users often open quick-commerce apps when they are:

* bored
* hungry but not truly hungry
* stressed
* lonely
* watching football/cricket
* gaming
* working late
* doomscrolling
* trying to reward themselves

They may order things they did not plan to buy. The next morning, they may regret:

* money spent
* calories eaten
* poor sleep
* breaking a diet
* unnecessary consumption
* feeling out of control

Existing solutions like habit trackers, calorie counters, and budgeting apps are too serious. They do not match the emotional state of a person craving chips at 1:30 AM.

DopeCart solves this with humor and simulation instead of discipline lectures.

## 7. Product Goals

### Functional Goals

* Let users browse parody shelf items.
* Let users add items to a cart.
* Let users get a satisfying checkout ritual experience.
* Show money saved, calories avoided, and regret avoided.
* Create shareable receipts.
* Encourage users to avoid real impulse ordering.
* Turn resisting cravings into a game.

### Emotional Goals

* Feel funny, not preachy.
* Feel familiar, not confusing.
* Feel like a late-night friend saying, “Bro, don’t do it.”
* Give the user a small reward for not ordering.
* Make self-control feel like a meme instead of a punishment.

### Business/Product Goals

* Create a viral MVP.
* Test whether users return after the initial joke.
* Build a retention loop using streaks, savings, and social sharing.
* Validate whether this can evolve into a habit-control, budgeting, or fitness-adjacent product.

## 8. Non-Goals

The MVP should not:

* Actually sell or deliver anything.
* Accept payment.
* Collect card/UPI details.
* Use Blinkit, Zepto, Instamart, Swiggy, or Zomato branding.
* Copy exact logos, UI, names, or assets from real apps.
* Mislead users into thinking they are placing real orders.
* Shame users for eating.
* Become a calorie-counting app in the first version.
* Require login in the first session.
* Become a full marketplace.

## 10. Core User Flow

### Flow 1: First-Time User

1. User lands on homepage.
2. App asks: “What are you craving?”
3. User sees categories:

   * Chips & Namkeen
   * Cold Drinks
   * Ice Cream
   * Chocolate
   * Maggi & Noodles
   * Momos
   * Biscuits
   * Random Midnight Purchases
4. User browses parody products.
5. User adds items to cart.
6. Cart shows cart total, calories, and “regret score.”
7. User taps “Place order successfully.”
8. App shows tracking ritual:

   * Order received
   * Rider assigned: Self Control
   * Packed by: Better Judgement
   * Delivery ETA: Never
9. Final screen:

   * “Order cancelled successfully.”
   * “You saved ₹486.”
   * “You avoided 1,240 calories.”
   * “You protected tomorrow morning.”
10. User gets a shareable receipt.

### Flow 2: Returning User

1. User opens app during craving.
2. App says: “Back again? What almost got you today?”
3. User builds a cart.
4. Checkout adds to:

   * Total money saved
   * Total calories avoided
   * No late-night order streak
5. User gets a badge or roast.
6. User can share the result.

### Flow 3: Emergency Craving Mode

1. User taps “I’m about to order for real.”
2. App starts a 10-minute craving timer.
3. During timer, app shows:

   * cart
   * memes
   * water reminder
   * “eat something basic if actually hungry”
   * “still hungry after 10 minutes? eat real food, not chaos”
4. At the end, user chooses:

   * “Craving passed”
   * “Still hungry”
   * “I ordered anyway”
5. App responds without shame.

## 15. Important Legal and Trust Constraints

The product should avoid impersonating real quick-commerce apps.

Do not:

* Use Blinkit, Zepto, Instamart, Swiggy, or Zomato logos.
* Copy their exact UI.
* Use confusingly similar brand names.
* Use real product images scraped from those apps.
* Suggest real delivery is happening.
* Ask for address or payment details.
* Use dark patterns that confuse users.

Do:

* Use original branding.
* Keep the parody-first stance clear.
* Use parody-style fictional products.
* Keep the parody nature clear before checkout.
* Avoid real payment flows.
* Make the product feel like entertainment/self-control, not deception.

Suggested footer:

> DopeCart is a parody self-control app for cart rituals and tiny trophies.

## 16. Ethical Design Principles

This app should reduce harmful impulse loops, not create a new addictive one.

Principles:

1. **No shame**

   * Do not make users feel bad for eating or ordering.

2. **No real payment**

   * Never collect payment details.

3. **No manipulative scarcity**

   * Avoid pressure countdowns that increase craving.

4. **Encourage actual food when needed**

   * If the user says they are genuinely hungry, suggest eating something simple and real.

5. **Reward stopping**

   * Make quitting the flow feel like winning.

6. **Avoid eating disorder triggers**

   * Calories should be optional or framed lightly.
   * Provide a toggle to hide calorie estimates.

7. **Do not over-optimize addiction**

   * The goal is short, satisfying sessions, not endless scrolling.

## 18. Success Criteria for MVP

The MVP is successful if:

* Users understand the product within 5 seconds.
* Users laugh or relate immediately.
* At least 40% of first-time users complete a checkout ritual.
* At least 10–15% share a receipt.
* Users return during late-night hours.
* Some users say it actually stopped them from placing a real order.
* Meme pages or creators can explain it in one screenshot.

## 20. Risks

### Risk 1: One-Time Meme

Users may try it once, laugh, and never return.

Mitigation:

* Add streaks.
* Add savings tracker.
* Add emergency craving mode.
* Make weekly summaries.

### Risk 2: Legal Issues

A too-close clone of Blinkit/Zepto/Instamart could create trademark or passing-off risk.

Mitigation:

* Original brand.
* Fictional products.
* Clear parody disclaimer.
* No copied logos or UI.

### Risk 3: It Becomes Too Preachy

Users do not want a lecture at 1 AM.

Mitigation:

* Humor first.
* Self-control second.
* No guilt-heavy language.

### Risk 4: It Triggers Food Shame

Calories and regret framing could be harmful for some users.

Mitigation:

* Optional calorie display.
* Focus on impulse and budget, not body shame.
* Add “actually hungry?” flow.

### Risk 5: No Clear Business Model

The product may become viral but not monetizable.

Mitigation:

* Treat MVP as validation.
* Build audience first.
* Later test habit, fitness, budgeting, and creator-led models.

6. As a user, I want streaks so that I can turn self-control into a game.

* Cart estimates calories avoided.

## 25. Open Questions

1. Should the app start as a web app or Android app?
2. Should calories be shown by default or hidden behind a toggle?
3. Should the tone be mostly English, Hinglish, or city-specific?
4. Should users be able to create custom parody products?
5. Should receipts be image-based for Instagram sharing?
6. Should the app allow anonymous city leaderboards?

## 26. Recommended First Version

The first version should be a mobile web app with no login.

Core flow:

Home → Browse Products → Add to Cart → Checkout → Tracking → Receipt → Share

The product should be funny within the first 10 seconds.

The main success moment should be:

> “I built a cart, laughed, did not order, and shared the receipt.”

If the MVP can create that moment reliably, the idea is worth expanding.
